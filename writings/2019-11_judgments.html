What does a judgment mean?

Zheng Shen

2019-11-4

This essay discusses the relation between a judgment and our interpretation of the judgment. In other words, we ask a participant about a sentence, the participant will give us a response, how do/can/should we (not) interpret that response in terms of acceptability?

I was taught and I have taught others that the purpose of syntactic theory is to come up with a rule system that can model human’s ability to distinguish a set of acceptable sentences from a set of unacceptable sentences.

In order to achieve that we need to know which sentences are acceptable and which ones are not. That’s why this enterprise is almost entirely driven by acceptability judgments. We get the responses from various acceptability judgment tasks, interpret them as labels such as ‘acceptable’ and ‘unacceptable’, then use these interpretations of responses as object of study in constructing analyses. On this essay I take issue with the link between the raw responses we get and the interpretation we give them.

An important disclaimer: I’m not even touching on where the acceptability comes from, maybe it’s grammar, maybe it’s processing, maybe it’s low frequency. I’m just talking about the (often) numerical response and the interpretation whether the sentence is acceptable or not.

OK here we go.

1. Individual sentences

If you ask a person to judge an individual sentence on either a two point (yes-no) or multiple point (3,5,7) scale, you will get a response x. What does x mean?

Ambiguity of small scales

End points on large scales are the best, but they are rare.

In some extreme cases where a sentence will get a 7 or 1, the relation between the response and the interpretation is uncontroversial. 7 means completely OK (acceptable), 1 means really really bad (unacceptable). I feel these interpretations of ends of scales are stronger as the number of the points increases. For example, I’m more comfortable to interpret 1 on the 7 point scale as definitely unacceptable than I am with a ‘no’ on a two point scale. If x=no on a two point scale, it might mean that the sentence is unacceptable, OR it might mean that the sentence is not perfect, which is a long way from unacceptable in people’s assumption.

The problem outlined above is sort of a first-world problem in the world of judgments. It can be solved in principle by always using a scale with many points. But that would only work if we live a world where every sentence is clearly a 1 or a 7. The fact is we rarely get 7 or 1 for our test sentences. Most of the judgments are somewhere in between. In this actual world, having too many points would make the numbers between end points meaningless. What would there be a difference between 3 and 10 on a 100 point scale?

The muddy middle ground

If a sentence is judged as in the middle of the scale, it is doomed.

Another more discussed problem occurs when the judgment is in the middle of the scale like 4 on a 7 point scale or a 3 on a 5 point scale. Some assume that when participants are not sure (possible grammatical gap), they would choose the middle point. That’s why some researchers use scale with even points to make sure there is no middle point (e.g. 6). But I don’t know how truly effective that is. Yes, you will have a number to work with, but that doesn’t mean it’s showing what you set out for it to show. Maybe the participants genuinely wanted to choose the middle point.

Root of the problem: linking hypothesis

We really don’t know what’s good and what’s bad.

Again, I’m NOT talking about the linking between acceptability and grammaticality. I’m talking about the linking between response and acceptability.

Above, we discussed the ambiguity involved with end points and middle points on a scale. What about other points? What does a 2 out of 7 mean?

Many researchers would say if a sentence is with a 2 out of 7 rating, it should be labeled unacceptable. But others would disagree. In almost all papers using a survey to collect judgments to calculate an average, you will find a statement to the effect of ‘we decided that sentences with a rating above x are acceptable; otherwise, unacceptable.’ Then they proceed to offer an account that would just generate the sentences above x and rule out sentences below x. This might give the readers an impression that x is something well supported.

It is not.

The decision where the cut-off should be is largely based on the authors’ intuition, if not downright arbitrary. Usually people go for the middle point, but I’ve seen papers deciding that anything above 1 (out of 5) is acceptable and 1 is the ugly child ruled out by the almighty grammar. The reason they gave is that all the test sentences in that section are in a construction with low frequency which has the low average anyways. But why not having 2 as the cut of point instead of 1? This equally plausible cut off point follows the same logic (below the average because of the low base rating), but end up with a different set of acceptable sentences and consequently a different analysis. There is a possible world where some author would first come up with predictions of a theory, and then decide the cutoff points based on the predictions. I imagine this wouldn’t be a good scientific practice. But we would not know if this happened because there is simply no agreed above guidelines on how to label responses. (On the other hand, given the low average of the construction, we wouldn’t know if some of the sentences rated as 1 are indeed unacceptable.)

Ideally, we would have some rules to label the response as acceptable/unacceptable which are independent from the theory, then develop an analysis that would model this dichotomy. Annoyingly, we don’t. So we really are just cutting the pie in whichever way that benefits us the most. If our interests are not compatible, we cut the pie differently and claim that our own way is the right one. The problem is not we are not ethical, the problem is we don’t know what to do even if we want to be ethical.

Note that this problem is already an “upgrade” from the old problem where one author claims a sentence is good (which happens to fit their analysis), and another author makes the opposite claim (which happens to fit their alternative analysis). Now it has become common practice to collect data from multiple people in an informal or a formal experimental setting, but we still suffer from this dire lack of acceptability compass.

Given this problem, it’s hard to see what our analyses account for, certainly not the objectively decided sets of acceptable/unacceptable sentences.

Maybe I’m just too much of a downer, I kind of hope that’s the case. Maybe you know that it is the case, that the bleak picture I painted above stems from my ignorance. If that’s the case, please teach me this: why did you choose x as the cutoff point instead of x-1 or x+1?

Someone please, save me.

But until then, I would take statements in the shape of ‘The average rating for this sentence is three, so I will take it as acceptable/unacceptable/borderline’ with a bunch of grains of salt.

Footnote: This last problem in section 1 is the worst in the minimalist program where sentences are assumed to be either generatable or “crashes”. Back in the GB and move alpha days, constraint based analyses are somehow milder and things are not as black and white. If sentence A violates some rule, it’s degraded. If it violates more rules, it’s even worse. In this framework, there is no magical cutoff point, which is why minimal pairs play a crucial role as is discussed in the next part.

2. Minimal pairs

OK let’s take a breath.

The problems presented above occur when responses of individual sentences are use as the raw materials for building an analysis. People have recognize the danger of that from early on, which is why many researchers emphasize the importance of minimal pair. The crucial thing about a minimal pair is that one acts as the baseline/control for the other.

Let’s say sentences A and B are the same but for one thing, i.e. minimally different. Let’s say A gets an average rating of x and B gets an x-3. If we test enough people with enough lexical variations, we can confidently say that A is better then B by 3 points on the scale. We might come up with a rule that can distinguish the two sentences based on the difference and we have a theory/analysis/account for the difference.

This is good news. We have clear results from comparison, based on which we can build a theory about the difference. Our theory would likely be a constraint the violation of which triggers a dispreference. To me, this kind of theory is much more believable than the one described in the previous section.

But note what the theory did not do. It did not account for acceptability of A or unacceptability of B. It did not do that because the response from judging a minimal pair does not give us the information regarding the absolute acceptability of A or B. The judgments based on minimal pairs did not put A, the better sentence, in the bin of acceptable sentences; or B, the worse sentence, in the bin of unacceptable sentences. In a theory driven by judgments of minimal pairs, there is no acceptable and unacceptable, only more acceptable and less acceptable. On the 7 point scale, maybe A is 7 and B is 4, or A is 4 and B is 1. The two sceanerios do not make a difference in the theory because the theory can only account for the difference, not the absolute acceptability.

As I argued above, the judgments of individual sentences do not put sentences in these bins either without an additional stipulation in the shape of ‘sentences judged x and above are acceptable’. Minimal pairs are no different in this regard. However, minimal pairs are superior in that they enable us to make conclusions regarding the difference. I do not know what indivdual sentences enable us to conclude.

The takehome message, for me at least, is that minimal pairs are great in providing us with data we can comfortably theoretize with. But the theories would be about the differences, not ‘good’ vs ‘bad’.

Footnote: More elaborated design can be built upon minimal pairs. For example, with 2 * 2 factorial designs, we get to compare sizes of differences between minimal pairs and detect interactions of multiple constraints.

I couldn’t help but conclude that neither judgements of individual sentences nor those of minimal pairs give us data that distinguishes acceptable sentences from unacceptable sentences. If a theory claims to account for the dichotomy, I’m curious how they decided where the dichotomy starts in the first place.

This summarizes my current (2019-11-4) thoughts/worries about how theoretical syntacticians deal with judgment data. Thanks for the comments and discussions, please keep them coming.
